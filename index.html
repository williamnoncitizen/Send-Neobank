The chasm to cross

Crypto natives are happy with Phase 0. Mainstream consumers will not switch from their bank until Send delivers the whole product they expect. Phase 1 is the chasm.

"Pragmatists do not adopt a product feature by feature. They wait for the whole product to exist, then switch all at once. The job is not to ship more features. The job is to ship the bundle that lets them leave their current bank." Crossing the Chasm, applied to Send

Ship next: top 5 by impact and what unlocks downstream work

Ordered by dependency, not feature count. Every gate below makes the next one cheaper.

1

Virtual card

Foundational and potentially faster via card-as-a-service. Unlocks Apple Pay, merchant acceptance, and card controls. Physical card follows later.

Crypto-card competitors often lead with virtual cards because it creates spend utility before a physical card program is fully scaled.

Missing Phase 1 High impact Low effort
2

Account & routing numbers

Enables payroll and direct deposit. Without it, every dollar enters Send via a competitor's product first.

Direct deposit is a common retention lever across neobanks. It gives users a reason to treat Send as a primary account instead of a pass-through wallet.

Missing Phase 1 High impact High effort
3

Apple Pay / Google Pay

Tap-to-pay in stores, one-tap online checkout. The feature that makes the card feel real on day one.

Standard across most card-led competitors. Effort drops once the card program is live, but partner approval and wallet enablement still matter.

Missing Phase 1 High impact Low effort
4

Neobank UX redesign

The whole-product unlock. Pragmatists won't trust their paycheck to an app that looks like a crypto wallet.

A banking-style interface is one of the clearest trust signals for mainstream users deciding whether to move paycheck, spend, and bill-pay behavior into Send.

Missing Phase 1 High impact Medium effort
5

Merchant card acceptance

Closes the spend loop. Card works at real merchants, not just Send-to-Send. Inherits from the virtual card program.

Once the virtual card program ships, merchant acceptance becomes the visible proof that Send can work beyond Send-to-Send transfers.

Missing Phase 1 High impact Low effort

Methodology

Gates and impact/effort calls are informed by the live comp analysis. The maturity ladder is the executive interpretation of those gaps, mapped to four readiness stages. The chasm framing applies Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore) to Send's transition from crypto-native to mainstream consumer product. Impact and effort scores are first-pass estimates by product and engineering, not committed dates or implementation commitments.

Sources

Competitor scores live in the comp analysis matrix. Twelve competitors evaluated: Send, Cash App, Phantom Cash, Ether.fi Cash, Tuyo, Avici, KAST, Revolut, Wise, Nubank, Brighty, Wirex. Twenty-five features across Identity, Account, Money Movement, Spend, Grow, and Trust. Scores are based on public pages reviewed for this analysis; some cells reflect announced, limited-availability, or region-specific features and should be rechecked before external use.

Yield, interest, APY, earn, and rewards references are informational only. Access may depend on variable rates, third-party providers, region, eligibility, and provider terms; capital may be at risk. SEND does not custody or guarantee third-party yields. Send is not a bank; banking-style capabilities may depend on licensed partners, jurisdiction, and approval.
